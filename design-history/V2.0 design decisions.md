# Version 2.0 Design Decisions

## 1. Forced Withdrawal Cannot Be Used to Attack Another City

As mentioned in the Version 2.0 rules, when a city's political alignment changes from **Level 1 to Neutral**, enemy military units stationed in that city are forced to withdraw.

During this forced withdrawal, the units must move to friendly territory. They are not allowed to use the withdrawal as an opportunity to immediately attack another city.

There are two main reasons for this decision.

First, from a thematic perspective, these units are being forced out of the city rather than actively launching a military operation. Therefore, it would not make sense for them to begin a new attack during the withdrawal process.

Second, this rule is important for game balance.

If attacking during withdrawal were allowed, players might deliberately manipulate the political system and allow their own units to be expelled in order to gain an additional opportunity to initiate combat.

This would make the relationship between political actions, retreat, and military movement unnecessarily complicated.

For this reason, forced withdrawal is treated purely as a retreat and cannot be used to begin a new attack.

---

## 2. Limiting Sacrifice Through Adjacent Garrisons to Reduce First-Player Advantage

Another issue identified during testing was first-player advantage.

To address this, Version 2.0 introduces a new restriction:

**A player may use Sacrifice to influence a city only if that city is directly connected to another city containing the player's military units.**

This rule makes actual military presence and geographical position more important.

The first player begins in the upper part of the map, while the second player occupies a more central geographical position.

Without this restriction, the second player's central location did not provide enough practical advantage because political influence could be applied too freely across the map.

By requiring nearby military presence, the map's geography and the player's actual area of control become more important.

This helps reduce the first-player advantage while allowing the centrally positioned player to make better use of their geographical position.

---

## 3. Repeated Struggle Between Level 1 and Neutral

One issue remains unresolved.

After the game develops for one or two rounds and the overall situation begins to stabilize, players may repeatedly compete over enemy cities between **Level 1** and **Neutral**.

Because political influence normally moves a city only one step at a time, the following pattern may occur:

1. One player moves an enemy city from Level 1 to Neutral.
2. Their military units enter or occupy the city.
3. During the following turn, the opposing player uses Sacrifice to influence the city again.
4. The occupying army is forced to withdraw.
5. The same city may then become the focus of repeated political and military conflict.

This is not necessarily a flaw in the system.

Repeated competition can represent an unstable political frontier, and repeated fighting may also cause additional **Damage** to the city.

Since players can choose where Damage is applied, the gradual destruction of a repeatedly contested city also creates additional strategic decisions.

However, if this pattern occurs too frequently, the game may become dominated by repetitive struggles between Level 1 and Neutral.

This could reduce gameplay variety and make long-term political development less important.

For this reason, the repeated struggle between Level 1 and Neutral remains an issue that requires further playtesting and observation.
