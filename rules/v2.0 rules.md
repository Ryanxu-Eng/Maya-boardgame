# Maya Board Game — Rules V2.0

## 1. Game Overview

*Maya Board Game* is a two-player strategy game inspired by the political rivalry between **Tikal** and the **Kaan dynasty** during the Classic Maya period.

Players compete through:

- Political influence
- Military expansion
- Resource management
- Sacrifice
- Control of city-states

Military occupation and political influence are two separate systems. A player may occupy a city militarily without fully controlling it politically, while a politically aligned city may also temporarily fall under enemy occupation.

The game lasts **7 rounds**.

In each round:

1. The **Kaan player** completes an entire turn.
2. The **Tikal player** then completes an entire turn.

After the Tikal player's turn in Round 7, the game ends and Victory Points are calculated.

---

## 2. Initial Setup

### 2.1 Starting Factions

The two players control:

- **Tikal**
- **Kaan / Calakmul**

Each player begins at their own capital.

### 2.2 Starting Units

Each player begins with:

- **3 Warriors**
- **1 Noble**

All starting units are placed in the player's capital.

### 2.3 Starting Resources

Each player begins with:

- **3 Food**
- **2 Wealth**
- **2 Labor**

There is no maximum limit on stored resources.

### 2.4 Starting Political Influence

The five ordinary city-states begin as follows:

| City | Starting Political Position |
|---|---|
| Palenque | Tikal L1 |
| Waka' | Kaan L1 |
| Dos Pilas | Kaan L1 |
| Naranjo | Neutral |
| Caracol | Neutral |

Tikal and Calakmul are capitals and do not have political tracks.

### 2.5 City Resources

| City | Resource Slots |
|---|---|
| Tikal | F / F / W / L |
| Calakmul | F / F / W / L |
| Palenque | F / W / L |
| Waka' | L / L / W |
| Dos Pilas | W / L / F |
| Naranjo | F / W / W |
| Caracol | F / F / L |

Where:

- **F = Food**
- **W = Wealth**
- **L = Labor**

---

## 3. Turn Sequence

Each player completes the following five phases in order.

### 1. Resource Phase

Collect resources from cities that the player is allowed to use.

#### Resource Collection

During each Resource Phase, the player who controls a city has one opportunity to choose which available resources are produced by that city.

The player may choose any resource or combination of resources shown on the city's Resource Slots, as long as the selected slots are not damaged.

For example:

- A player may choose to produce **Labor** from a city during one turn.
- During the next Resource Phase, the player may instead choose to produce **Wealth** from the same city.

If a player is allowed to use two or three Resource Slots in a city, the player may choose any combination of available resources from those slots.

All selected Resource Slots must be undamaged.

Damage remains attached to the specific slot where it was placed until that Damage is removed through repair.

### 2. Political Phase

The player may perform Sacrifice actions in multiple different cities.

For each individual city:

- That city may be targeted by Sacrifice only once during the same player's turn.
- The player may use either **Ordinary Sacrifice** or **Grand Sacrifice** on that city, but not both.

Ordinary Sacrifice and Grand Sacrifice may both be performed during the same turn as long as they target different cities.

### 3. Military Phase

The player may:

- Move military units
- Enter cities
- Attack enemy forces
- Attack Neutral or politically hostile cities

Each military unit has a movement value of **1**.

### 4. Upkeep Phase

Pay Food according to the total number of ordinary Warriors currently controlled.

Nobles do not require upkeep.

### 5. Recruitment and Reconstruction Phase

The player may:

- Recruit Warriors
- Recruit a Noble
- Repair city Damage

Units recruited during this phase cannot move until the player's next turn.

---

## 4. Resource Costs

### 4.1 Political Influence Costs

A Sacrifice moves one city's political track by one step toward the acting player.

| Political Shift | Cost |
|---|---|
| Neutral ↔ L1 | 1 Food + 1 Wealth |
| L1 ↔ L2 | 1 Food + 2 Wealth |
| L2 ↔ L3 | 1 Food + 1 Labor + 1 Wealth |

When reducing enemy political influence, the cost is determined by the level being weakened.

For example:

- Enemy L3 → Enemy L2: **1F + 1L + 1W**
- Enemy L2 → Enemy L1: **1F + 2W**
- Enemy L1 → Neutral: **1F + 1W**

### 4.2 Warrior Recruitment

One Warrior costs:

**1 Food + 1 Labor**

Warriors may be recruited in:

- The player's own capital
- An unoccupied friendly L2 city
- An unoccupied friendly L3 city

There is no limit to the number of Warriors recruited in one turn, provided the player can pay the cost and does not exceed the unit limit.

### 4.3 Noble Recruitment

One Noble costs:

**2 Wealth + 1 Labor**

A Noble may only be recruited in:

- The player's own capital
- An unoccupied friendly L3 city

A player may recruit a maximum of **1 Noble per turn**.

Each player may have a maximum of **3 Nobles**.

A newly recruited Noble enters play in the **Ready** state.

### 4.4 Army Upkeep

Only ordinary Warriors count toward upkeep.

| Number of Warriors | Food Cost |
|---|---:|
| 0–1 | 0 |
| 2–4 | 1 |
| 5–7 | 2 |
| 8–10 | 3 |
| 11–13 | 4 |
| 14 | 5 |

Nobles do not increase upkeep cost.

If a player cannot pay the required Food, they must remove Warriors until the remaining army can be supported by the Food they are able to pay.

#### Army Disbandment

A player may only begin disbanding units if their current amount of Food is insufficient to pay the required army upkeep.

When disbanding units:

**For every 2 units removed, the player gains 1 Food.**

If an odd number of units is removed, the final unpaired unit provides no additional Food.

The amount of Food gained is therefore calculated as:

**Number of units disbanded ÷ 2, rounded down**

Examples:

- Disband 2 units → Gain 1 Food
- Disband 3 units → Gain 1 Food
- Disband 4 units → Gain 2 Food
- Disband 5 units → Gain 2 Food

The player may continue disbanding units until the upkeep cost of the remaining army can be paid.

Once the player's remaining army has reached a level where the upkeep can already be paid, the player may still continue to disband additional units.

Any additional units removed continue to provide Food according to the same rule.

### 4.5 Repairing Damage

Repairing one point of Damage costs:

**2 Labor**

The player chooses one damaged slot and restores it.

### 4.6 Noble Ransom

The ransom price for one captured Noble is:

**3 resources**

Only the player holding the Noble may initiate a ransom offer.

Once the captor formally offers the Noble for ransom, the offer cannot be withdrawn.

The original owner may:

- Accept
- Refuse

If the offer is accepted, the Noble's original owner chooses the exact combination of three resources used as payment.

For example:

- 3 Food
- 2 Food + 1 Labor
- 1 Food + 1 Wealth + 1 Labor

Any combination is allowed, and the captor must accept the chosen combination.

If the offer is refused, the Noble remains captive.

Ransom does not restore an Exhausted Noble to Ready status.

---

## 5. Cities and Political Influence

### 5.1 Ordinary Cities

The five ordinary cities are:

- Palenque
- Waka'
- Dos Pilas
- Naranjo
- Caracol

Each ordinary city has:

- 3 Resource Slots
- 2 Defense Slots
- 1 Political Track

The political track is:

**Kaan L3 — Kaan L2 — Kaan L1 — Neutral — Tikal L1 — Tikal L2 — Tikal L3**

### 5.2 Political Levels

#### Level 1

The aligned player may:

- Use 1 undamaged Resource Slot

The player may not recruit units there.

#### Level 2

The aligned player may:

- Use 2 undamaged Resource Slots
- Recruit Warriors

The city also provides **+1 Political Support** during a city battle for the politically aligned side.

#### Level 3

The aligned player may:

- Use all 3 undamaged Resource Slots
- Recruit Warriors
- Recruit Nobles

The city also provides **+2 Political Support** during a city battle for the politically aligned side.

#### Neutral

A Neutral city provides no political benefits or Political Support.

---

## 6. Sacrifice and Political Actions

### 6.1 Ordinary Sacrifice

During the Political Phase, a player may perform Ordinary Sacrifice in multiple different cities.

Each individual city may be targeted by Sacrifice only once during the same player's turn.

The target city's political track moves one step toward the acting player.

The appropriate resource cost must be paid.

### 6.2 Political Connectivity

A city may only be targeted by Sacrifice if it has at least one direct city-to-city connection to:

- The player's own unoccupied capital

or

- A city at the player's own L1, L2 or L3 that is not under enemy military occupation

A city without such a connection is considered **Isolated**.

An Isolated city cannot be targeted by:

- Ordinary Sacrifice
- Grand Sacrifice

Political influence does not automatically decline because a city is Isolated.

### 6.3 What Does Not Provide Political Connectivity

The following do not count:

- Neutral cities
- Enemy-aligned cities
- Friendly political cities currently occupied by the enemy
- Blank military regions
- Friendly armies standing in blank military regions

Only direct connections between valid friendly cities count.

### 6.4 Occupied Cities and Sacrifice

A player may not perform Sacrifice in a city that is currently militarily occupied by the opposing player.

### 6.5 Military Presence Requirement for Sacrifice

A player may use Sacrifice to influence a city only if at least one city directly connected to the target city contains that player's military units.

This means political influence must be supported by a nearby military presence.

A player cannot use Sacrifice to influence a distant city while bypassing the surrounding area.

---

## 7. Grand Sacrifice

A captured enemy Noble may be used to perform a Grand Sacrifice.

The target city must:

1. Be militarily occupied by the acting player.
2. Have previously been captured through military action.
3. Have remained continuously occupied.
4. Still have its political track on the enemy side.
5. Not be Isolated.

A Grand Sacrifice counts as the Sacrifice action for that specific city during the turn. The same city cannot also receive an Ordinary Sacrifice during that turn.

The political track moves **2 steps toward the acting player**.

The normal costs of both steps are added together.

The sacrificed Noble replaces any **2 resource points** from the total cost.

After being sacrificed, the Noble returns to the original owner's supply and may later be recruited again.

---

## 8. Capitals

The two capitals are:

- Tikal
- Calakmul

Capitals do not have political tracks.

They always fundamentally belong to their original faction.

Each capital has:

- 4 Resource Slots
- 4 Defense Slots

The original owner may normally use all undamaged Resource Slots.

### 8.1 Occupying an Enemy Capital

While an enemy army occupies a capital:

- The original owner cannot collect resources from it.
- The original owner cannot recruit there.
- The occupying player may use a maximum of 1 undamaged Resource Slot.
- The occupying player cannot recruit there.

If all occupying enemy units leave, the capital immediately returns to the control of its original owner.

### 8.2 Capital Plunder

Whenever a player successfully captures the enemy capital, the attacker chooses one resource type:

- Food
- Wealth
- Labor

The attacker takes **all resources of that type** currently held by the defending player.

This effect may occur again if the capital is later recaptured and then captured again.

Capitals do not use the normal **Pillage City** rule.

---

## 9. Military Spaces

The map contains two kinds of military spaces:

- Cities
- Blank military regions

Blank military regions are the open military areas formed by the map's city connections.

The connection lines themselves are not military spaces.

Unmarked areas outside the playable map are not military spaces.

---

## 10. Movement

Each military unit has:

**Movement 1**

Each unit may move a maximum of once during its player's Military Phase.

Units may move together or split into separate forces.

A unit may move:

- Between directly connected cities
- Between a city and an adjacent blank military region
- Between adjacent blank military regions where the map treats them as connected military spaces

Entering a space containing enemy military units immediately causes a battle.

Entering an unoccupied Neutral city or an unoccupied politically hostile city causes a city battle against the city's local defenses.

---

## 11. Military Occupation

Political influence and military occupation are separate.

A city is militarily occupied only while at least one enemy military unit remains inside it.

When the final occupying unit leaves, the occupation immediately ends.

Political influence does not automatically change because of military occupation.

### 11.1 Resource Use During Occupation

#### Occupied Enemy-Aligned City

The occupier may use:

**1 undamaged Resource Slot**

The occupier may not recruit there.

#### Occupied Neutral City

The occupier may use:

**2 undamaged Resource Slots**

The occupier may not recruit there.

#### Occupied City Reaching the Occupier's L1

The occupier may use:

**2 undamaged Resource Slots**

The occupier may not recruit there.

#### Occupied City Reaching the Occupier's L2

Normal L2 rules apply:

- Use 2 Resource Slots
- Recruit Warriors

#### Occupied City Reaching the Occupier's L3

Normal L3 rules apply:

- Use all 3 Resource Slots
- Recruit Warriors
- Recruit Nobles

### 11.2 Forced Withdrawal When Political Alignment Becomes Neutral

If a city's political alignment changes from Level 1 to Neutral, and military units belonging to the opposing faction are currently stationed inside that city, those units must immediately withdraw.

The owner of the withdrawing army chooses the retreat destination.

The army may retreat to:

- An adjacent blank military region
- A friendly city-state

If there is no legal retreat destination, the player may remove:

**1 Warrior**

After removing that Warrior, the remaining army may be moved directly to:

- Any friendly city
- The player's own capital

---

## 12. Pillage City

Pillage applies only when successfully capturing an ordinary city.

The sequence is always:

### 1. Pillage

The attacker chooses one currently undamaged Resource Slot in the city.

The attacker immediately gains **1 resource of that type**.

### 2. Damage

After Pillage is resolved, the city suffers **1 Damage**.

The newly caused Damage may be assigned to the same Resource Slot that was just Pillaged.

Therefore, the sequence must always be:

**Pillage first → Damage second**

A player may not apply Damage first and then attempt to Pillage.

If every Resource Slot in the city was already damaged before the capture, no resource can be Pillaged.

---

## 13. Damage

All cities may have a maximum of **3 Damage**.

Whenever a city changes military control because of a successful capture, it suffers **1 Damage**.

The new military controller chooses one currently undamaged functional slot to disable:

- A Resource Slot

or

- A Defense Slot

A damaged Resource Slot:

- Produces no resource
- Cannot be selected for Pillage

A damaged Defense Slot provides no defensive strength.

A failed attack does not cause Damage.

Field battles do not cause Damage.

---

## 14. Units

### 14.1 Total Unit Limit

Each player may have a maximum of **14 total military units**.

This includes:

- Warriors
- Nobles

A player may have no more than **3 Nobles**.

Captured Nobles still count toward:

- Their original owner's Noble limit
- Their original owner's total 14-unit limit

Destroyed Warriors return to the player's supply and may later be recruited again.

---

## 15. Noble

Each Noble is a military unit.

A player may have a maximum of **1 Noble in the same military force or military space**.

### 15.1 Noble Combat Strength

A Noble fighting alone has:

**1 Combat Strength**

A Noble fighting with at least one Warrior has:

**2 Combat Strength**

Therefore:

- 3 Warriors + 1 Noble = 5 base strength
- 1 Warrior + 1 Noble = 3 base strength
- 1 Noble alone = 1 base strength

### 15.2 Noble Command Ability

Each Noble has two states:

- Ready
- Exhausted

The ability may only be used during a **city battle**.

It cannot be used during a field battle.

Before dice are rolled:

1. The attacker declares first whether their Noble will use the ability.
2. The defender then decides whether their Noble will use the ability.

#### Only One Noble Uses the Ability

Both players roll normally.

The player who activated the Noble uses **the higher of the two dice results** as their own final die result.

The opposing player keeps their own original result.

#### Both Nobles Use the Ability

The abilities cancel each other.

Each player uses their own original die result.

Both Nobles still become Exhausted.

A Noble does not automatically return to Ready each round.

The following do not restore the ability:

- Captivity
- Ransom

Only leaving the game and later being newly recruited causes the Noble to return in the Ready state.

---

## 16. Combat

There are two types of battle:

- Field Battle
- City Battle

Each side rolls **1D6**.

### 16.1 Base Combat Strength

Each Warrior provides:

**1 Combat Strength**

A Noble provides:

- 1 when alone
- 2 when accompanied by at least one Warrior

### 16.2 City Defense

During a city battle, the defending side also receives:

**+1 Combat Strength for each undamaged Defense Slot**

If the city contains no player-controlled defending army, the city still defends itself using its undamaged Defense Slots and applicable Political Support.

### 16.3 Political Support

In a city battle:

| Political Level | Support |
|---|---:|
| L1 | +0 |
| L2 | +1 |
| L3 | +2 |

Political Support always belongs to the politically aligned side.

It does not automatically belong to the current military occupier.

### 16.4 Total Combat Strength

Each side calculates:

- Base Unit Strength
- Defense Slots, if applicable
- Political Support, if applicable
- Final Dice Result

The player with the higher total wins.

### 16.5 Ties

#### Field Battle

A tie is won by the **Defender**.

#### City Battle

A tie is won by the **Attacker**.

---

## 17. Combat Casualties

Casualties are resolved in the following order.

### Step 1: Dice Casualties

A final effective die result of:

- 1–4: causes no casualty
- 5–6: causes the opponent to lose 1 Warrior

These losses are simultaneous.

It does not matter who wins the battle.

### Step 2: Defeat Casualty

The losing side loses **1 additional Warrior**.

### Step 3: Major Defeat

If the difference between the two final Combat Strength totals is **4 or more**, the losing side loses **1 additional Warrior**.

Nobles are never removed as normal combat casualties.

---

## 18. Retreat

After casualties are resolved, the losing force must retreat.

A legal retreat destination may be:

### Blank Military Region

Only if it contains no enemy units.

### Friendly City

The city must:

- Contain no enemy military units

and be:

- The player's own capital

or

- A city at the player's own L1, L2 or L3

Neutral cities and enemy-aligned cities are not valid city retreat destinations.

### 18.1 No Adjacent Legal Retreat

If the losing force has no adjacent legal retreat:

1. It loses **1 additional Warrior**.
2. The remaining force may retreat directly to any friendly city or its own capital anywhere on the map, provided the destination is not enemy-occupied.

This represents the cost of breaking out of encirclement.

---

## 19. Noble Capture

After all battle casualties and retreat-related casualties have been resolved:

If a defeated Noble has no accompanying Warrior remaining, the Noble is captured by the opposing player.

A Noble that fights alone and loses is normally captured.

### 19.1 Neutral City Exception

When a player attacks a Neutral city and loses:

**The attacking Noble is never captured.**

The Noble retreats instead.

This rule applies regardless of the other casualties suffered during the battle.

Neutral city defenses therefore cannot hold Noble prisoners.

---

## 20. Victory Points

After the Tikal player's turn in Round 7, calculate Victory Points.

### 20.1 Political Influence VP

For each ordinary city:

| Political Position | VP |
|---|---:|
| L1 | 1 |
| L2 | 3 |
| L3 | 5 |
| Neutral | 0 |

### 20.2 Prosperity VP

The following cities may score Prosperity VP:

- The player's own capital, if not enemy-occupied
- The player's own L2 city, if not enemy-occupied
- The player's own L3 city, if not enemy-occupied

VP depends on Damage:

| Damage | VP |
|---|---:|
| 0 | 2 |
| 1 | 1 |
| 2–3 | 0 |

### 20.3 Final Military Occupation VP

An ordinary city that is politically not aligned with the player but remains militarily occupied by that player at the end of the game is worth:

**1 VP**

An enemy capital that remains occupied at the end of the game is worth:

**5 VP**

### 20.4 Captured Noble VP

Each enemy Noble still held captive at the end of the game is worth:

**1 VP**

### 20.5 Army VP

For ordinary Warriors still controlled by the player:

**Every 2 Warriors = 1 VP**

Round down.

Examples:

- 1 Warrior = 0 VP
- 2 Warriors = 1 VP
- 5 Warriors = 2 VP
- 10 Warriors = 5 VP

Each recruited Noble still controlled by the player and not held captive is worth:

**1 VP**

### 20.6 Remaining Resource VP

Add together all remaining:

- Food
- Wealth
- Labor

For every **3 remaining resources = 1 VP**.

Round down.

---

## 21. Final Score

A player's final score is:

- Political Influence VP
- Prosperity VP
- Military Occupation VP
- Captured Noble VP
- Army VP
- Remaining Resource VP

The player with the higher total wins.

If both players have the same total, the game ends in a draw.
